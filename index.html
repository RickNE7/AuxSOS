Business Letter Review

Contractual Agreement & Delivery Terms Analysis

Gianella Armas Rosamía Céspedes Marilin Medina Eric Mozombite Tamia Obregón

1. Misused Phrasal Verbs

Get off the ground Commence / Initiate

Definition: To start or begin a project or activity formally.
Example in Context: "Our company is eager to commence the new partnership."
Spanish Equivalent: Iniciar / Comenzar
Justification: "Get off the ground" is informal. "Commence" ensures professional suitability for corporate agreements.

Iron out Resolve / Address

Definition: To find a solution to a problem or settle a difficulty.
Example in Context: "...this collaboration will allow us to resolve the problems that have delayed our expansion."
Spanish Equivalent: Resolver / Solucionar
Justification: Legal writing requires objective verbs. "Resolve" provides clarity over the colloquial "iron out".

Carry out with Comply with

Definition: To act in accordance with a rule, order, or schedule.
Example in Context: "Finally, the contract requires us to comply with the delivery schedule."
Spanish Equivalent: Cumplir con / Acatar
Justification: "Carry out with" is grammatically incorrect. "Comply with" accurately defines legal adherence.

2. Misused Idioms

Touch base Communicate / Meet

Definition: To briefly make or maintain contact with someone.
Example in Context: "...our team will communicate with your representatives every two weeks."
Spanish Equivalent: Contactar / Reunirse
Justification: A sports idiom is inappropriate. "Communicate" explicitly defines the required formal interaction.

Ball is in your court Pending your action

Definition: Awaiting a decision or step from the other party.
Example in Context: "...which states that dispute resolution is pending your action."
Spanish Equivalent: Depende de usted / Está en sus manos
Justification: This cliché ruins the serious tone of legal documentation. "Pending your action" is precise.

Break the ice Establish rapport

Definition: To create a harmonious and professional connection.
Example in Context: "We want to establish rapport with your company from the beginning."
Spanish Equivalent: Establecer un buen vínculo
Justification: Literal translations of "break the ice" cause confusion in Spanish. "Rapport" is professionally accurate.

3. Technical & Legal Terms (Part 1)

Force major Force majeure

Definition: Unforeseeable circumstances that prevent someone from fulfilling a contract.
Example in Context: "First, the clause on force majeure seems incorrectly applied."
Spanish Equivalent: Fuerza mayor
Justification: An incorrect spelling (anglicization) of the established French legal term severely alters professional credibility.

Bill of landing Bill of lading

Definition: A detailed list of a ship's cargo in the form of a receipt given by the master of the ship.
Example in Context: "Second, the reference to the bill of lading is inaccurate..."
Spanish Equivalent: Conocimiento de embarque
Justification: A common but fatal typo in logistics. "Lading" (from loading) is the required terminology for maritime transport.

3. Technical & Legal Terms (Part 2)

Arbitrage clause Arbitration clause

Definition: A contract provision requiring parties to resolve disputes via an arbitrator rather than court.
Example in Context: "We also found the use of an arbitration clause which changes the meaning entirely."
Spanish Equivalent: Cláusula de arbitraje
Justification: "Arbitrage" means trading assets in different markets. "Arbitration" is the correct legal method for dispute resolution.

Memorando de ent. obligatorio MOU (Non-binding)

Definition: A formal agreement between two or more parties that is not legally binding.
Example in Context: "...translates it incorrectly as binding, which misrepresents its non-binding nature."
Spanish Equivalent: Memorando de entendimiento (no vinculante)
Justification: Translating MOU as "obligatorio" creates a severe legal misrepresentation, as MOUs are fundamentally non-binding.
